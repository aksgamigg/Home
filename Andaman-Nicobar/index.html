Flora of Andaman & Nicobar

Dense tropical evergreen forests, mangroves, littoral vegetation, and rare plants form the base of all life in these islands.

Tropical evergreen backbone

Rainforests, timber trees & rich undergrowth

Most of the Andaman & Nicobar Islands are covered by tropical evergreen and semi-evergreen forests, with tall canopy trees, dense undergrowth, and multiple layers of vegetation.[web:15][web:16]

Valuable timber species like Andaman padauk and various hardwoods grow here, along with palms, bamboo, ferns, and orchids that thrive in the warm, humid climate.[web:16][web:18]

Where land meets sea

Mangroves, littoral forests & sandy beaches

Along creeks, estuaries, and sheltered coasts, mangrove forests stabilize the shoreline, trap sediments, and provide nurseries for fish, crabs, and other marine life.[web:16][web:20]

Littoral (coastal) vegetation includes pandanus, casuarina, and hardy shrubs that can tolerate salt spray, wind, and shifting sands on beaches and dunes.[web:20]

Endemic & rare species

Plants found only in these islands

The islands host many endemic plant species, including Andaman padauk and several palms and shrubs restricted to this region.[web:15][web:18]

Rare plants like the Andaman pitcher plant, a carnivorous species, and wild orchids highlight how isolation has allowed unique flora to evolve here.[web:21]

Fauna of Andaman & Nicobar

From endemic mammals and colorful birds to coral reefs, turtles, and reef sharks, the islands support over nine thousand animal species.

Endemic mammals & rich birdlife

Mammals, reptiles & 270+ bird species

The islands host about 46 species of mammals, including endemics like the Andaman wild pig, Nicobar flying fox, Andaman horseshoe bat, and several shrews and tree shrews.[web:15][web:18][web:21]

Over 270 bird species occur here, with around 90 endemic forms such as the Nicobar pigeon, Andaman woodpecker, Andaman drongo, and the Nicobar megapode that builds large nest mounds on the ground.[web:17][web:21][web:22]

Underwater diversity

Coral reefs, turtles, sharks & dugongs

The surrounding seas support vibrant coral reefs with over 200 coral species and more than 1,300 species of molluscs and echinoderms, plus a huge variety of reef fish.[web:17][web:19]

Larger marine animals include dugongs (the state animal), sea turtles such as leatherback, green, hawksbill and olive ridley, dolphins, whales, and several reef shark species.[web:19][web:21]

Endangered & endemic

Why conservation here really matters

Many species in these islands are listed as vulnerable or endangered, including the dugong, Nicobar megapode, leatherback turtle, and several endemic mammals and reptiles.[web:20][web:21]

Because a large share of the fauna is endemic, losing habitat or disturbing nesting beaches and coral reefs can mean global extinction for some species.[web:17][web:25]

Conservation & Protected Areas

Large parts of the islands are protected as national parks, wildlife sanctuaries, and a biosphere reserve to safeguard fragile ecosystems.

Threats: habitat loss, climate change, tourism pressure Protection: national parks, biosphere reserve, coastal regulations

Great Nicobar Biosphere Reserve

The Great Nicobar Biosphere Reserve covers most of Great Nicobar Island and includes Campbell Bay and Galathea National Parks as its core areas.[web:20][web:29]

It protects tropical evergreen forests, mangroves, coral reefs, and key species like the Nicobar megapode, Nicobar macaque, Nicobar tree shrew, saltwater crocodile, and nesting leatherback turtles.[web:20][web:29]

Why this matters for India

Andaman & Nicobar act as a biodiversity bridge between the Indian, Indo‚ÄëChinese and Indo‚ÄëMalayan regions, adding unique species and ecosystems to India‚Äôs natural heritage.[web:25][web:26]

For students, these islands are an excellent case study of how forests, coasts, and oceans are linked‚Äîand why conservation has to consider local people, wildlife, and climate together.[web:20][web:24]
